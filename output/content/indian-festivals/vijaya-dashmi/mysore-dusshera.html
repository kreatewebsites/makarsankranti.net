The Vijayanagara Origins

The grand Dasara festivities, marked by royal splendor, are thought to have originated in the Vijayanagara Empire during the 15th century. Foreign chroniclers, such as Domingo Paes, documented the elaborate 'Mahanavami' celebrations at Hampi. Key traditions like military parades, regal processions, and the veneration of the state sword and throne took root in this period, influencing later dynasties.

Wodeyar Dynasty's Adoption

Following the fall of the Vijayanagara Empire, Raja Wodeyar I of Mysore reinstated the tradition in Srirangapatna in 1610. Recognizing the festival's cultural and political importance, he formalized the ritual of ascending the throne and hosting a grand durbar, embedding Dasara as a key symbol of Mysore's sovereignty and heritage.

Interruption and Revival

The Dasara celebrations paused under Hyder Ali and Tipu Sultan's rule. After Tipu Sultan's death in 1799, the British restored the Wodeyar dynasty. Krishnaraja Wodeyar III, the new ruler, moved the capital to Mysore and revived the Dasara festival with enthusiasm, aiming to restore his kingdom's cultural prominence.

The Grand Durbar

Under Krishnaraja Wodeyar III, Dasara evolved into a grand spectacle. The festival allowed the king to connect with his people, accept tributes from vassals, and showcase the state‚Äôs military and cultural splendor. The iconic Vijayadashami procession, which later became the Jumboo Savari, rose to prominence during his reign, establishing Mysore as the heart of Dasara festivities.

Transition to a State Festival

Following India's independence and the end of princely states, the festival's fate hung in the balance. The Government of Karnataka stepped in to ensure its continuation, safeguarding its cultural significance while modernizing it for a democratic society. Declared the 'Nadahabba' or State Festival, the event evolved under state sponsorship. The royal family maintains its private rituals, but the grand public procession now serves as a vibrant celebration of Karnataka's heritage.

Your Visitor's Guide

Embarking on a journey to experience Mysore Dasara promises excitement and wonder. This guide offers vital details and handy tips to enhance your visit. From the ideal time to go to maneuvering through the festive crowds, these pointers aim to deliver a smooth and unforgettable celebration at the heart of the festivities.

Dasara spans ten days in the Hindu month of Ashvin, usually in September or October. The grandest day is Vijayadashami. Be sure to confirm the dates for the year you plan to attend. Secure your travel and stay early, as the city becomes very busy.

During Dasara, several city-center roads near the palace are closed for the procession. Walking or using public transport is recommended. Auto-rickshaws and app-based cabs are options but may cost more due to surge pricing. Exploring on foot offers the best festive experience.

  • Gold Card: The Karnataka State Tourism Development Corporation (KSTDC) offers a 'Gold Card' granting access to key Dasara events and attractions, letting you skip lengthy lines.
  • Palace Illumination: The palace lights up from 7 to 10 PM. Come early to grab a great photo spot.
  • Jumboo Savari Route: The parade follows a 5 km path from the palace to Bannimantap. Arrive early to secure a great viewing spot.
  • Local Cuisine: Be sure to sample Mysore's renowned sweets like Mysore Pak and its flavorful savory dishes on your trip.
